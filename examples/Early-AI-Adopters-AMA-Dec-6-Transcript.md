## On Fine-Tuning vs RAG

**Speaker:** We lost our UX writer this week, and I've got a whole heap of content that I want to—I'll cut straight to the chase. I was going to fine-tune an LLM with our company tone and voice, just so it's specifically writing like our UX writer would. Is it worth it?

And I've watched all of the RAG or fine-tuning stuff, and I'm still like, I've just never done fine-tuning. So I'm like, I'll do it for the exercise and for the learning, more so than because it's needed.

**Mark:** By the way, you can just do fine-tuning and ask it to build the training data out of the UX person's writing, and then it could train itself, make the model, and you can use the model. If you want to go down that route, it is totally possible without N8N and having to do open heart surgery.

**Speaker:** There's no harm in doing the fine-tuning on the model, then using that model with the RAG, because the fine-tuning is the tone, the voice.

**Mark:** That's a system prompt you can apply. That's a prompting issue. You would just take the essence of all the UX writer's style and ask AI to synthesize that in the form of a prompt.

Because now as you add a fine-tuned model with RAG, when something goes wrong, you have five different vectors for where that thing could be wrong. Meaning when you start reverse engineering where the thing that's screwed up is—it could be the model, it could be the prompt for the model, it could be the RAG, it could be the chunk. You just increase the overhead.

**Speaker:** All right, thanks. You talked me out of it.

**Taha:** This is a very big mistake many big companies make, so listen to Mark—this is valuable advice.

**Mark:** It's crazy how many times I have to convince them not to fine-tune. But companies come from a machine-learning background, so that's all they do—model work. And then you're going to be further from the money because you're going to be wasting time on fine-tuning.

Fine-tuning is a machine-learning derivative concept. It comes from this idea of what's called transfer learning, where back in the day for image recognition, you would borrow Google's machine-learning model's brain because it was already trained on billions of images, and you'd use that and build on top of it.

So when people say fine-tuning, a lot of engineering teams from before get super excited because it reminds them of machine learning, even though it's the wrong application for the right use case.
